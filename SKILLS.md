# 🚀 Lean Startup Milestone Map & Action Plan

> **Who is this for?** Anyone who has an idea and wants to turn it into a real business — without wasting years and money building the wrong thing. No prior knowledge required. Every term is explained in plain English.

> **What is this?** A step-by-step playbook used by world-class companies like Airbnb, Uber, and Facebook to go from "just an idea" to a thriving product. It's broken into four phases, each with clear goals, actions, checklists, and real examples.

## Phase 1: Problem-Solution Fit
### *The "Desirability" Check — Does Anyone Actually Care?*

> **The Big Question:** Is the problem you're solving real, painful, and frequent enough that people will pay for a solution?

Most startups fail not because they built something bad, but because they built something *nobody wanted*. This phase is about making sure you're solving a real problem before writing a single line of code or spending a single dollar.

---

### 🎯 Goal

**Confirm that "Customer Pain" genuinely exists.**

You want to find people who are frustrated enough with a problem that they've already been trying to solve it themselves — even with clumsy, imperfect workarounds. *That frustration is gold.*

---

### 🗺️ Action Plan

**1. Customer Discovery — Talk to Real People (20+ Interviews)**

Go out and have honest conversations with potential customers. The golden rule: **do not pitch your idea**. Just listen. Ask them about their current frustrations, what tools they already use, and how much time or money the problem costs them.

- Schedule 20–30 short, 30-minute interviews (coffee chats, video calls, etc.)
- Ask open-ended questions like: *"Walk me through the last time you had this problem. What did you do?"*
- Take notes on exact words and phrases they use — this language will become your marketing copy later.

> 💡 **Why 20+ interviews?** One or two conversations might be flukes. Twenty conversations start to reveal real patterns. If 15 out of 20 people say the same thing, you've found a real insight.

**2. Concierge MVP — Do It Manually First**

Before automating anything, do the service *by hand* for a small group of users. Pretend to be "the software." This reveals exactly where the friction, confusion, and value actually live.

- Pick 5–10 willing test users and serve them manually (e.g., if you want to build a meal-planning app, send them a manual meal plan via email).
- Observe where they get confused or frustrated, and where they light up with excitement.
- You will discover what the product *actually* needs to do, not just what you *think* it needs.

> 💡 **The Concierge MVP gets its name** from hotel concierges who provide a custom, human-powered service. You're doing the same thing — manually — before you invest in automation.

**3. Landing Page Test — Measure Real Intent**

Build a simple one-page website that describes the problem you're solving and offers a "Join the Waitlist" or "Pre-order Now" button. Drive a small amount of targeted traffic (friends, social media, a small ad budget) and see how many people actually click.

- Use free tools like Carrd, Webflow, or even a Google Form.
- Include a clear headline describing the pain point (not your solution), a brief description, and a single call-to-action button.
- Track how many visitors click the button vs. just leave. This is your **conversion rate**.

> 💡 **Why this works:** People *saying* they want something and people *giving their email* for something are very different things. A landing page test reveals real intent.

---

### 🏆 Success Metric

**Conversion rate (visitors → sign-ups) exceeds 5–10%.**

If you drive 100 targeted, relevant people to your landing page and 5–10 of them sign up, you have a signal worth pursuing. If it's 0–2%, something is wrong — either the audience, the message, or the problem itself.

---

### 🌍 Real-World Example: Airbnb

In 2008, Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia had a simple, almost embarrassing idea: rent out air mattresses in their San Francisco apartment to conference attendees who couldn't find a hotel.

They didn't build an app first. They built a *basic* website, posted photos, and **manually managed bookings via email**. This is the Concierge MVP in action.

What they discovered: strangers *would* pay to stay in someone else's home. That single validation was worth more than a year of building an app. The full Airbnb platform came *after* this proof.

**Key lesson:** Airbnb validated the problem (strangers need affordable, local accommodation) and the solution (staying in someone's home) *before* investing in technology.

---

### ⚠️ Anti-Patterns / Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Skipping interviews and jumping straight to building** | You'll spend months (and thousands of dollars) building something nobody asked for | Do the 20+ interviews first, always |
| **Leading the witness in interviews** | Asking "Would you use an app that does X?" will get you false "yes" answers | Ask about the *past* ("Tell me about the last time..."), not hypotheticals |
| **Treating friends' opinions as market research** | Friends want to be supportive, so they'll tell you what you want to hear | Talk to *strangers* who match your target customer profile |
| **Perfecting the landing page design** | You're delaying the actual test; a beautiful page doesn't equal a real customer | Launch an ugly page in one day; iterate based on real data, not aesthetics |
| **Giving up after 5 interviews** | 5 interviews is not a large enough sample; patterns only emerge at scale | Commit to at least 20 before drawing conclusions |

---

### ✅ Phase 1 Checklist

- [ ] Defined my target customer: *(who specifically has this problem?)*
- [ ] Written a one-sentence problem statement: *(e.g., "Freelancers struggle to track client payments without expensive accounting software")*
- [ ] Completed 20+ customer discovery interviews
- [ ] Documented recurring pain points and exact quotes from interviews
- [ ] Built and launched a landing page (even a rough one)
- [ ] Driven at least 100 targeted visitors to the landing page
- [ ] Achieved 5%+ conversion rate on the landing page, **OR** identified why the rate is low and iterated
- [ ] Completed at least one Concierge MVP experiment with 5–10 real users
- [ ] Documented learnings from the Concierge MVP (what surprised you?)
- [ ] Confident the problem is real, painful, and frequent enough to build a business around

## Phase 2: Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
### *The "Viability" Check — What's the Smallest Thing You Can Build?*

> **The Big Question:** What is the *absolute minimum* set of features that deliver the core value to your earliest customers?

You've confirmed the problem is real. Now you need to build *something* — but the trap most founders fall into is building too much. An MVP is not a "basic version" of your final product. It is a **learning tool** designed to test your most important assumptions as fast as possible.

---

### 🎯 Goal

**Find the "Must-Have" feature set — the features users can't live without.**

---

### 🗺️ Action Plan

**1. Feature Prioritization — The MoSCoW Matrix**

Before building anything, sort every potential feature into four buckets. This prevents "feature creep" — the tendency to keep adding things until the product never ships.

| Priority | Label | Definition | Example (for a budgeting app) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 🔴 **Must-Have** | M | Without this, the product doesn't work | Connect to bank account, show spending |
| 🟡 **Should-Have** | S | Important but not critical for launch | Budget categories, monthly summaries |
| 🟢 **Could-Have** | C | Nice but easily cut | Dark mode, custom color themes |
| ⚫ **Won't-Have (this time)** | W | Explicitly out of scope for now | AI-powered investment advice |

Build *only* the Must-Have features for your MVP. Everything else comes later.

**2. Closed Beta — Hand-Pick Your First 10–50 Users**

Don't launch publicly yet. Instead, personally invite a small group of "Early Adopters" — people who are passionate about the problem, willing to use imperfect software, and able to give you articulate feedback.

- Find early adopters in communities where your target customer hangs out (Reddit, Slack groups, LinkedIn, local meetups).
- Onboard them personally — get on a video call and watch them use your product for the first time.
- The goal is not growth; the goal is *learning*. You want quality feedback, not quantity.

> 💡 **"Early Adopter" mindset:** These users don't expect perfection. They want to be first, they want to influence the product, and they're willing to trade convenience for being ahead of the curve.

**3. Feedback Loops — Set Up Automated Learning Systems**

Instead of waiting for users to spontaneously tell you what's wrong, set up systems that actively collect feedback.

- **NPS Survey (Net Promoter Score):** After a user has used the product for a few weeks, send a single-question survey: *"On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend this product to a friend?"* Scores of 9–10 are "Promoters"; scores of 0–6 are "Detractors."
- **Sean Ellis "Product/Market Fit" Survey:** Ask: *"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"* with answer choices: Very Disappointed / Somewhat Disappointed / Not Disappointed. The benchmark is 40% "Very Disappointed."
- Set up simple in-app event tracking (e.g., with Mixpanel or Amplitude) to see which features users actually click on vs. ignore.

---

### 🏆 Success Metric

**40% or more of surveyed users say they would be "Very Disappointed" if they could no longer use the product.**

This threshold was identified by entrepreneur Sean Ellis after surveying hundreds of startups. Companies that hit 40%+ consistently went on to achieve strong growth. Companies below 40% consistently struggled. Think of it as a "heartbeat" for whether your product is truly valuable.

---

### 🌍 Real-World Example: Facebook

When Mark Zuckerberg launched "The Facebook" in February 2004, he didn't launch it to the whole world. He launched it **only to Harvard University students**.

This was a deliberate MVP strategy:

- The audience was small and controlled (a single campus)
- The feedback loop was tight (he could walk around and watch people use it)
- The risk was minimal (if it failed, it failed small)

Within 24 hours, 1,200 Harvard students had signed up. Within a month, half the undergraduate population had joined. Only after proving the product worked at Harvard did Zuckerberg expand to other universities — Yale, Columbia, Stanford — one at a time.

**Key lesson:** Facebook didn't try to conquer the world in one day. It proved the product worked in the smallest possible context first, then expanded.

---

### ⚠️ Anti-Patterns / Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Building too many features** | You waste time on things nobody asked for, delay launch, and confuse users | Ruthlessly apply the MoSCoW matrix; if in doubt, cut it |
| **"Stealth mode" — building for 12+ months without user feedback** | You're flying blind; the market will have moved on or you'll have built the wrong thing | Ship to 10 users within 6–8 weeks, no matter how rough |
| **Launching to everyone at once** | You can't have meaningful conversations with 10,000 users; you need intimacy | Keep the beta small (10–50 users) so you can talk to each one |
| **Asking "What features do you want?" in surveys** | Users will give you a wish list, not insight into real problems | Ask "What would you miss most if this product disappeared tomorrow?" |
| **Ignoring the 40% benchmark** | Optimizing for metrics that don't correlate with real success | Run the Sean Ellis survey early and often; treat 40% as your north star |

---

### ✅ Phase 2 Checklist

- [ ] Listed every possible feature idea in a backlog
- [ ] Applied the MoSCoW matrix; identified the Must-Have features
- [ ] Built *only* the Must-Have features for the first version
- [ ] Identified and personally contacted 10–50 early adopters
- [ ] Onboarded early adopters via video call and watched them use the product
- [ ] Set up NPS survey (auto-sends after 14–21 days of use)
- [ ] Set up Sean Ellis "Product/Market Fit" survey
- [ ] Set up in-app event tracking to see which features are actually used
- [ ] Reviewed survey results with 20+ responses
- [ ] Achieved 40%+ "Very Disappointed" score, **OR** identified specific product gaps to fix
- [ ] Documented the top 3 reasons users love the product and the top 3 reasons they don't

## Phase 3: Product-Market Fit
### *The "Retention" Check — Do People Come Back?*

> **The Big Question:** After users try your product once, do they come back? And do they stick around for months?

Getting users to try your product is relatively easy (run an ad, ask a friend). Getting them to *come back* is the real test. If you have strong retention, you have Product-Market Fit (PMF). If users churn (leave and don't return), you don't — no matter how many people sign up.

---

### 🎯 Goal

**Prove people stay, come back, and would genuinely miss the product if it disappeared.**

---

### 🗺️ Action Plan

**1. Cohort Analysis — Track Retention Over Time**

A "cohort" is a group of users who joined in the same time period (e.g., "everyone who signed up in January"). Cohort analysis answers the question: *Of all the users who joined in Month 1, what percentage are still active in Month 3? Month 6?*

- Set up a simple retention tracking tool (Mixpanel, Amplitude, or even a Google Sheet).
- Define what "active" means for your product (e.g., "logged in at least once in the last 30 days" or "completed a core action").
- Plot a "retention curve." A healthy curve *flattens out* above zero (some users keep coming back). A dying product's curve drops to zero (everyone eventually leaves).

> 💡 **What you're looking for:** If you sign up 100 users in January, you want at least 20–30 of them still active in March. That's a 20–30% "Day-60 retention rate" — a strong signal of PMF for many product categories.

**2. Pricing Strategy — Find the Value-Cost Sweet Spot**

If users love the product but nobody will pay for it, you don't have a business. This step is about finding the price where the value you deliver clearly outweighs what you charge.

- Run a simple pricing test: offer the same product to different groups at different price points (e.g., $9/month vs. $19/month vs. $29/month).
- Ask churned users *why* they left — if price is rarely mentioned, your pricing may actually be *too low* (counter-intuitive but true).
- Use the "Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter" — ask users: *"At what price would this feel too expensive? At what price would it feel like a bargain?"*

> 💡 **Pricing insight:** Many early-stage founders undercharge because they're afraid of rejection. But cheap pricing can actually *hurt* you — it attracts low-quality customers who churn fast and never give useful feedback.

**3. Unit Economics — Make Sure the Math Works**

You need to understand, at a fundamental level, whether your business model makes money. The two key numbers are:

- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** How much money does it cost you to acquire one new paying customer? (Total marketing/sales spend ÷ number of new customers)
- **LTV (Lifetime Value):** How much revenue does one customer generate before they stop paying? (Average monthly revenue per customer × average number of months they stay)

The goal is **LTV > 3 × CAC** — meaning you make at least $3 for every $1 you spend to acquire a customer.

---

### 🏆 Success Metric

**LTV > 3 × CAC** (Customer earns back at least 3x what it cost to acquire them)

For example: If it costs you $30 to acquire a customer (through ads, sales time, etc.), that customer should generate at least $90 in revenue over their lifetime with your product.

---

### 🌍 Real-World Example: Uber

When Uber expanded into new cities, they didn't just flip a switch and hope for the best. They obsessively tracked **rider retention** after each city launch.

- They tested **dynamic pricing** (surge pricing): Would riders still use Uber when prices were higher? The data showed that a majority of riders accepted moderate surge pricing — proof that the core value (reliable, convenient rides) outweighed the cost increase.
- They ran **cohort analyses** for each city: Were riders from Week 1 still using Uber in Week 8? Cities where retention was strong got more investment; cities where it was weak got product and operational fixes first.
- They tracked **CAC vs. LTV** aggressively: Early driver and rider incentives (like free rides) were only justified if the long-term LTV of acquired riders exceeded the acquisition cost.

**Key lesson:** Uber's expansion playbook was built on unit economics and retention data, not just raw growth numbers.

---

### ⚠️ Anti-Patterns / Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Optimizing for signups, not retention** | Signups are vanity metrics; retention is the real signal of value | Track what % of Month-1 users are still active in Month-3 |
| **Ignoring churned users** | They hold the most valuable feedback — they left for a reason | Interview churned users first; ask the single question "Why did you stop?" |
| **Assuming the pricing is fine without testing** | You may be leaving money on the table or pricing out good customers | Run a structured pricing experiment with at least 3 price points |
| **Scaling before achieving PMF** | Pouring money into growth when you have a leaky bucket just accelerates your death | Refuse to run growth experiments until your Day-60 retention is healthy |
| **Ignoring unit economics** | A business can look healthy (lots of users!) while fundamentally losing money | Calculate LTV/CAC before every significant spending decision |

---

### ✅ Phase 3 Checklist

- [ ] Defined what "active user" means for my product (specific action, not just "logged in")
- [ ] Set up cohort-based retention tracking
- [ ] Achieved a healthy Day-30 retention rate (industry-specific; aim for 20–40%+ for SaaS)
- [ ] Retention curve has *flattened* (not still declining to zero)
- [ ] Identified the top reasons users churn (interviewed at least 10 churned users)
- [ ] Run a pricing experiment with at least 2–3 different price points
- [ ] Calculated my CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- [ ] Calculated my LTV (Lifetime Value)
- [ ] Confirmed LTV > 3 × CAC
- [ ] Product team has fixed the top 3 reasons users were churning
- [ ] Confident that a paying, retained customer segment exists and the math is healthy

## Phase 4: Scaling
### *The "Growth" Check — Pour Fuel on the Fire*

> **The Big Question:** Now that you have a "machine" where $1 invested yields $3+ in return, how do you grow as fast as possible without breaking what works?

Scaling too early is one of the top startup killers. Scaling *after* you've proven the machine works is how you build a lasting company. This phase is about turning a manually operated, somewhat-fragile product into a robust, automated, rapidly-growing business.

---

### 🎯 Goal

**Achieve repeatable, scalable, and predictable growth.**

---

### 🗺️ Action Plan

**1. Channel Optimization — Find Your 1–2 Growth Engines**

There are dozens of ways to acquire customers (SEO, paid ads, referrals, partnerships, content marketing, sales, PR, etc.). Most don't work for your specific business. Your job is to run small, cheap experiments across several channels and *identify the 1–2 that work best*, then invest heavily in those.

- Run a 30-day experiment on 3–4 channels simultaneously with a small budget ($500–$1,000 total).
- Track the CAC for each channel separately.
- Cut channels that are expensive or slow, and "double down" (10x your investment) on the 1–2 with the lowest CAC and highest LTV customers.

> 💡 **The "rule of 1–2 channels":** Most great companies are built on one primary growth channel. Facebook = virality. Airbnb = SEO + Craigslist hack. LinkedIn = email invites. Find *yours*.

**2. Operational Hardening — Automate What's Manual**

In the early stages, you handled many things manually (customer support, billing, onboarding, reporting). As you grow, these manual processes become bottlenecks that slow you down and introduce errors.

- Document every manual process your team currently performs.
- Identify which manual tasks consume the most time per unit of growth.
- Hire for the specific *bottleneck role* first (e.g., if customer support is overwhelming you before growth, hire a support person; don't hire a growth marketer yet).
- Automate repetitive tasks using tools like Zapier, n8n, or custom scripts.

> 💡 **"Bottleneck hiring":** The best companies don't hire generically ("we need more people"). They identify the single biggest thing slowing them down and hire precisely for that.

**3. Infrastructure — Build for 10× Your Current Scale**

At some point, your "scrappy" early tech stack will break. Your servers will go down, your database will get slow, and your team will spend more time fixing bugs than building features. Plan for this *before* it becomes a crisis.

- Conduct a simple "scaling audit": What breaks first if your user base grows 10×? 100×?
- Invest in automated testing, monitoring/alerting (e.g., PagerDuty, Datadog), and horizontal scaling (cloud infrastructure that can expand automatically).
- Migrate from "founder-built, fast and dirty" code to "engineered, documented, maintainable" code — incrementally, not all at once.

> 💡 **The key is *incremental* investment:** You don't need to rebuild everything at once. Fix the highest-risk components first, and build a culture of reliability as you grow.

---

### 🏆 Success Metric

**Month-over-Month (MoM) revenue or user growth of 15% or higher, sustained for 6+ consecutive months.**

A 15% MoM growth rate means you roughly double every 5 months. Sustained for a year, that's nearly 5× growth. This is the benchmark that top venture capitalists use to identify companies on a path to $1B+ in value ("unicorn" territory).

---

### 🌍 Real-World Example: Airbnb

After Airbnb validated their core concept (Phase 1) and built their platform (Phase 2), they hit a growth wall. Their biggest competitor was Craigslist, where people already listed and searched for short-term rentals.

So they built a (now-famous) **Craigslist integration hack:**

- When a host listed a property on Airbnb, the system offered to automatically cross-post the listing to Craigslist with a single click.
- The listing on Craigslist linked back to Airbnb, driving traffic from a massive existing platform to their small new one.

This was one specific growth channel (Craigslist cross-posting) that worked extraordinarily well. They also launched a **referral program** (give $25 credit, get $25 credit) that became a second powerful growth engine.

**Key lesson:** Airbnb didn't try to grow through 10 channels at once. They found 1–2 specific channels that worked and invested heavily in those — growth through focused experimentation, not scattershot spending.

---

### ⚠️ Anti-Patterns / Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Hiring aggressively before finding growth channels** | You'll burn your runway before figuring out what actually works | Identify and validate your top growth channel before scaling headcount |
| **Optimizing for growth before PMF** | You're filling a leaky bucket — all that acquired growth will churn away | Only begin Phase 4 after Phase 3's LTV > 3× CAC is confirmed |
| **Ignoring unit economics at scale** | What worked cheaply at 100 customers may be unprofitable at 10,000 | Recalculate CAC and LTV every quarter as you grow |
| **Rebuilding the entire tech stack at once** | A massive rewrite is high-risk and slow; it delays features and can introduce new bugs | Incrementally refactor; fix what breaks, don't rebuild what works |
| **Treating all growth channels equally** | Spreading budget thin across 10 channels means none of them get enough investment to work | Run small tests, identify winners, then go all-in on 1–2 channels |

---

### ✅ Phase 4 Checklist

- [ ] Confirmed LTV > 3× CAC from Phase 3 before starting growth investment
- [ ] Ran small experiments on at least 3–4 growth channels
- [ ] Identified the 1–2 channels with the best CAC and highest-quality customers
- [ ] Doubled down on top channels (10× budget vs. experiment budget)
- [ ] Documented all current manual processes in the business
- [ ] Identified the single biggest operational bottleneck
- [ ] Automated or delegated the top 3 most time-consuming manual processes
- [ ] Conducted a scaling audit of the tech infrastructure
- [ ] Addressed the top 1–2 infrastructure risks before they become crises
- [ ] Achieved 15%+ MoM growth for at least 3 consecutive months
- [ ] Sustaining 15%+ MoM growth for 6+ consecutive months

## Key Terms Glossary

> All the jargon you encountered in this document, explained in plain English.

---

**MVP (Minimum Viable Product)**
The smallest, most basic version of your product that still delivers the core value to users. It's not a buggy, half-finished product — it's a *focused* product that does *one thing* well enough to test your assumptions.

---

**Lean Startup**
A methodology for building businesses by continuously testing assumptions with real customers instead of spending years planning in isolation. Popularized by Eric Ries in his book *The Lean Startup*. The core loop is: Build → Measure → Learn → Repeat.

---

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**
The total cost to acquire one new paying customer. Formula: Total Marketing & Sales Spend ÷ Number of New Customers Acquired. Example: If you spend $1,000 on ads and acquire 50 customers, your CAC is $20.

---

**LTV (Lifetime Value)**
The total revenue a single customer generates over their entire relationship with your business. Formula (simplified): Average Monthly Revenue per Customer × Average Number of Months They Stay. Example: If a customer pays $10/month and stays for 12 months, their LTV is $120.

---

**NPS (Net Promoter Score)**
A single-question survey: *"On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend this product to a friend or colleague?"* Scores of 9–10 = "Promoters," 7–8 = "Passives," 0–6 = "Detractors." NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors. A score above 50 is considered excellent.

---

**MoSCoW Method**
A prioritization framework for features (or requirements). Stands for: **M**ust-have, **S**hould-have, **C**ould-have, **W**on't-have (this time). Helps teams ruthlessly focus on what's essential vs. what's nice-to-have.

---

**Cohort Analysis**
A way of grouping users by when they joined (e.g., "January cohort" = everyone who signed up in January) and tracking how their behavior changes over time. Answers questions like: "Of the users who joined 3 months ago, what % are still active today?"

---

**Retention Curve**
A graph that shows what percentage of users from a specific cohort are still active after 1 week, 1 month, 3 months, etc. A curve that *flattens above zero* means you have retained users (good). A curve that *drops to zero* means you eventually lose all your users (bad).

---

**Unit Economics**
The analysis of revenue and cost *per unit* — usually per customer. Instead of asking "Is the whole company profitable?", unit economics asks "Does making one more sale earn us money or lose us money?" The key ratio is LTV / CAC. A ratio above 3 is healthy.

---

**Product-Market Fit (PMF)**
The state where your product deeply satisfies a specific market segment's needs — users love it, recommend it, and would be devastated if it disappeared. The term was coined by venture capitalist Marc Andreessen. It's often described as a "feeling" (things go well without forcing it) as much as a metric.

---

**Concierge MVP**
A product validation technique where you manually perform the service for a small group of users instead of building software to automate it. Named after hotel concierges who provide personal, customized service. The goal is to learn what users actually need before investing in automation.

---

**Early Adopter**
A customer who is willing to use your product before it's polished, in exchange for being "first" and having influence over the product's direction. They have a high tolerance for bugs and imperfection. They are invaluable for learning, but they are *not* representative of mainstream customers.

---

**Sean Ellis Survey**
A single survey question: *"How would you feel if you could no longer use [Product]?"* with three answer choices: Very Disappointed / Somewhat Disappointed / Not Disappointed. The benchmark for Product-Market Fit is 40%+ answering "Very Disappointed." Created by entrepreneur and growth expert Sean Ellis.

---

**Churn**
The rate at which customers stop using or paying for your product. If you have 100 customers and lose 5 per month, your monthly churn rate is 5%. High churn = users don't find enough value to stay. Reducing churn is almost always more valuable than acquiring new customers.

---

**Growth Channel**
A specific, repeatable way of acquiring new customers. Examples: SEO (getting found on Google), Paid Ads (Google Ads, Facebook Ads), Referrals (existing users invite friends), Content Marketing (blog posts, videos), Sales (a human calls and closes deals), Partnerships (another company refers customers to you).

---

**Dynamic Pricing**
A pricing strategy where the price changes in real-time based on supply and demand. Uber's "surge pricing" is the most famous example: when demand for rides exceeds available drivers, prices automatically increase to attract more drivers and reduce demand.

---

**MoM (Month-over-Month) Growth**
The percentage increase in a key metric (usually revenue or active users) from one month to the next. Formula: (This Month − Last Month) ÷ Last Month × 100. A 15% MoM growth rate means the business doubles in size roughly every 5 months.

---

**Bottleneck**
In a business context, the single step or resource that limits the overall speed of the entire system. If your customer support team is overwhelmed and preventing you from onboarding new users, support is your bottleneck. The theory of constraints says: *fix the bottleneck first, nothing else matters*.
